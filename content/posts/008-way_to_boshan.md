Being an avid podcast listener for a decade has the consequence of suppressing it to the background and not paying that much attention to content. Luckily, this time I was in an exploration mood and came across a conversation with Tamás Sajó that triggered something.

<!--more-->

Apart from being extremely knowledgeable, Sajó is the most humble scholar I've ever heard. He can speak about any topic because of his vast knowledge while doing it with the utmost respect. He is an art historian by trade who worked at the Hungarian Academy Of Sciences, and teaches about middle age history and applied information sciences at CEU. After breaking free from the academy, he founded his own publishing company, Studiolum. He translates books, holds online seminars, and organizes thematic trips. According to the "Az élet, meg minden" podcast, he speaks at least 15 (fifteen!) languages and can make himself understood by 15 others.

In one interview, he talked about a Chinese poem by Xin Qiji and the intricacies of translating these poems into any other language. The piece itself and his interpretation both struck me. Before jumping to conclusions, here is the poem itself in Chinese, English and Hungarian side-by-side:

[^1]: Translation by Xu Yuanchong. Taken from Yuan Jiayin's and Wang Feng's [article](https://oapub.org/lit/index.php/EJALS/article/view/371/401) in the European Journal of Applied Linguistics Studies, Volume 5, Issue 1.

The tension between the two verses culminates at the end of them. It comes from the similarity and stark contrast between autumn (秋, qiū) and despair (愁, chóu). Fall is the season of burnbaiting, and that is what its logogram represents. Reaching the end of the natural cadence of things, we come to a clean - yet calm - state. On the other hand, we have autumn's symbol expanded with the heart (心, xīn) character, which altogether means sadness. The first verse is about the "false despair" of young age and the willingness to force oneself to write about it, while the second speaks about the pure admiration of a cold autumn without the need to talk about anything else. Maybe this is Xin Qiji's way of distancing himself from the kitsch of poetry written about false feelings and the inexperienced younger self. Who can blame him? Life is about steering clear of bloat and focusing on what matters.

About ten years ago, I wrote "poems" about sadness, but in retrospect, they speak about false despair. I'm going through a lot of self-reflection lately, which has the same conclusion as the words of Xin Qiji. Maybe that's why they say that Chinese poems are pinpoint accurate.
