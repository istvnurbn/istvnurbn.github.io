I visit local cafés at least two times a month and usually purchase beans from Hungarian micro roasteries. I was mostly interested in filter roasts but recently I've stumbled upon a blend specifically for the Moka pot in unusual packaging.

<!--more-->

![KOTTY blend in its can packaging.](/images/posts/002.jpg "KOTTY blend in its can packaging.")
 
After a while, I decided to give it a try and purchased one *can*. As far as I know, Lucky Cap Roastery is the first one from Hungary that has a plantation in Lourdes, Costa Rica. Coffee coming from that place goes under the "Bányai" branding and the above-mentioned one is no exception.
 
They have very specific instructions on how to prepare it, even down to the filtered water that you should use. I already prepare drinks in the Moka pot in a very specific way so this was no surprise, but it brought a smile to my face nonetheless.
 
To be honest, I'm always skeptical about pre-ground coffee, but this was one of my top five drinks made with that method. They promise a spicy dark chocolate taste with subtle dried plum undertones. I definitely felt the first but the fruitiness was absent. This is most probably due to the fact, that my taste perception is far from ideal and I do not really go above and beyond on taste profiling.
 
Anyway, this endeavor triggered something in me to be more conscious about the whole coffee experience and it is better to articulate my thoughts than to keep them to myself. That was one of the reasons I decided to start blogging again. In light of the above, I repurposed my Moleskine notebook into a diary where I will keep a log of the coffees I taste and my opinion on them. Some of that might end up here as well.

kotty
: Short for *kotyogós*, the Hungarian nickname of the Moka pot.
